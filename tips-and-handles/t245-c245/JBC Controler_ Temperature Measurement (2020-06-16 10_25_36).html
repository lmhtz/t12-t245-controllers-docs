JBC Controler: Temperature Measurement

I'm continuing a series of articles on JBC station construction. The subject of this article is to measure the tip temperature. To do this, we use metallurgy, electronics, MySQL, Excel and, of course, mathematics.

 

Temperature measurement at JBC ends

There is a thermocouple located in the ends with the heater element. Although the subject of this thermocouple has some detail in itself, one of the connecting ways is:

The terminals + and - are connected to both the heating element and the thermocouple. So you can heat the tip or read the temperature, the two can not be done at the same time. (For C245 series leads you can read from C1 and C2)

Reading the thermocouple

Thermocouple (thermocouple) is a material which is produced by the combination of two different types of metal and reacts by producing voltage to the applied temperature. Its physical work depends on the principle of ”Seebeck effect Fiziksel; We invite you to Wikipedia.

An important point that many people jumped is; you can read all the electronic and mathematical calculations you can read a thermocouple without a precise temperature value. The thermocouple gives a relative value relative to the cold junction. So the thermocouple actually reads the temperature difference, does not give exact temperature. If you want to achieve precise temperature, you must also put the cold junction into the account. The ambient temperature + TC will give you the exact temperature.

There are two main challenges to the use of this material. The first one is the electronic part and the other one is mathematical.

 

I said the thermocouple produced a voltage in response to the temperature. But this voltage (for example K type thermocouple) is a low voltage, such as 41 okV for 1 degree Celsius. You need to upgrade it (to fit your ADC range for maximum sensitivity) and read it so. You'll understand that 200+ gain opamps come into play. I'm going to discuss how I do it in the sequel.

 

Thermocouple connection in mathematical terms

When you need to turn the voltage given by the Thermocouple to centigrade, mathematics comes into play. In simple terms, there are types of thermocouples available on the market and there are voltage / temperature curves according to these types. The most commonly used type K, as I wrote above, corresponds to a 41 artışV increase of about 1 degree centigrade. I'm saying because the thermocouples don't give linear output. 41 orV simplifies the account, perhaps it will be enough for most jobs, but when you need to read a sensitive value, the job gets messy.

 

NIST ITS-90 Thermocouple Database

On the page accessible from http://srdata.nist.gov/its90/main/ , you will find tables prepared by NIST (American Institute of National Standards and Technology, TSE). For example  , when you go to http://srdata.nist.gov/its90/type_k/0to300.html , you will find a list of the voltage values ​​of a K-type thermocouple in the range 0-300C. If you want to make a sensitive reading, you should take advantage of the values ​​in this table. If you say how you will benefit, either linear interpolation, curve fitting, or using polynomial coefficients given by NIST, you can obtain data with accuracy of 0.06C for model K type.

 

But what kind of thermocouple you have?

It is unclear what type of thermocouple is used in the JBC ends. In this case NIST tables do not help us, the business is falling off. We try to reference a reference to a JBC terminal by referencing it with a known measurement.

 

To do this,

The JBC handle and one end are attached at the front, there is some solder to increase the heat transfer at the tip, there is also a thermocouple embedded in this solder ball. I know this is the K type of thermocouple that looks connected with this white cable. Therefore it is possible to read a relative value by making an account book. I told you that I could not account for the inside of the soldering iron tip, we are trying to come up to the point where we can calculate it by taking the other thermocouple account. The thermocouple I use is rather dandy (the ones that get out of the chimneys), but I only have this.

There is also the circuit that runs the soldering iron and the thermocouple in the tip, the MAX6675 I use to facilitate the connection of the K-type thermocouple behind it, and the Arduino to read all this. The terminal program called CoolTerm, which is responsible for logging a computer and logging, is open for logging the readings and then paving the way for me to process it. Since it takes around 8A at 14V, one automotive type fuse can be seen in the photo because it says that the table does not catch fire.

 

Software running on Arduino; the tip gradually heats up to 460C and takes samples from the thermocouple inside the tip as well as the thermocouple we touch from the outside and writes to the terminal. The value we read for JBC is between 0-1024 (10 bit ADC resolution), and the value we read from the thermocouple is in degrees Celsius. In this way, I'm bypassing the JBC thermocouple voltage to ADC value.

I install it here for those who want to see the output when the system is running. CoolTerm Capture 2016-07-03 18-24-28

 

I ran the procedure 3 times in order to get the average. I have created a MySQL database and I've buried the files. After an SQL reference table was ready.

This is done in Excel, but I don't know, I know, readers are expected to comment in the field.

 

 

Of course, life has never been so easy, at high temperatures, my deviations have arisen. For more than 200 times, I sampled and added it to the database. I then read the data I read in Excel and drew the trend line. The red line in the Scatterplot is our trend. We have a second-order polynomial on our account.

The reason why the plot is so dispersed is my measurement technique and electrical noise. The flying cables and the MAX6675 SMD, which are connected to the TC, are connected to the Arduino socket with 30AWG modwire.

 

Thanks to the polynomial equation that Excel brings to us, we can now calculate how many degrees of the ADC values ​​0-1000 correspond to. The only thing missing in the formula is cold junction. So far we have never taken into account; At the end of the formula, the constant in the collection process means already room temperature (not exactly, but it does). An ambient temperature reading from an extra sensor can be entered into this account. When I was making the measurements, the room was at a temperature of 30 degrees.

If we do not want to make a polynomial calculation on the microcontroller, we can draw 0-1024 and take 100 samples, for example, using linear interpolation. I did not decide which way to use it yet, there was no hardware multiplier in the PIC, but this application and it will work on ATMega32, so it can be preferred. It will be converted from ADC to C for 10+ times per second, which will be preferred if our microcontroller has less interference. I get stuck with it because MCU is also able to produce interrupt-based PWM generation connected to PID account, OLED screen riding, rotary encoder reading, 32x ADC oversampling and so on. he is busy with business.
